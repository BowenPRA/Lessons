The Economy and the U.S. Government

For Vietnamese ESL Students
The Role of Government
Although the United States has primarily a free market economy, the U.S. government has important economic functions. The executive and legislative branches set the federal budget. The budget may have a surplus (revenues exceed expenditures) or a deficit (expenditures exceed revenues, requiring the government to borrow money).
The government promotes the general welfare by providing infrastructure (like the Interstate Highway System) that indirectly supports economic activity. It also redistributes wealth through taxes and social benefits.
Protecting Workers and Consumers
  • OSHA (Occupational Safety and Health Administration): Protects workers from hazardous conditions on the job.
  • FDA (Food and Drug Administration): Ensures food is safe, and drugs/cosmetics are effective and labeled properly.
  • FTC (Federal Trade Commission): Ensures that product claims in advertising are true and complete.

Fiscal vs. Monetary Policy

Both policies affect the amount of money circulating in the economy, but they are controlled by different entities.
Policy Type (Loại chính sách) Who Controls It? (Cơ quan quản lý) How It Works (Cách hoạt động)
Fiscal Policy
(Chính sách Tài khóa)		 The Government
(Executive & Legislative branches)		 Increases or decreases taxes and spending to influence the economy.
Monetary Policy
(Chính sách Tiền tệ)		 The Federal Reserve "The Fed"
(The central banking system)		 Increases or decreases the supply of money and interest rates to stimulate or slow the economy.

Labor and Consumer Issues

Workers: As workers, we earn wages and benefits. A worker's total compensation is the combined value of wages and benefits (like health insurance or vacation pay). In our modern economy, rapid changes often cause job losses, requiring workers to continually learn new skills. In some industries, labor unions use collective bargaining to negotiate contracts for all members.
Consumers: The free market offers amazing choices, but consumers must protect themselves. Although government agencies (like the FDA and FTC) help, consumers should remember the maxim: "Let the buyer beware." This means consumers must read labels carefully and understand credit terms to spend their money wisely.
