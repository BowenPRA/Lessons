The Divided Nation: The American Civil War

Historical Reading Passage
Focus on American History & Civic Principles
1
In the mid-1800s, the United States was a rapidly growing country, but it was deeply divided. Instead of feeling like one united nation, the country was split into two very different regions: the North and the South. People in these regions had different lifestyles, different economies, and different beliefs about how the government should work. These intense disagreements eventually led to the American Civil War (1861-1865), the deadliest conflict in U.S. history, fought between Americans.
2
The biggest difference between the North and the South was their economy, specifically the issue of slavery. The North had an industrial economy. This means people lived in large cities and worked in factories that produced goods like clothes, shoes, and tools. The North did not rely on slave labor. In contrast, the South had an agricultural economy based on farming. The Southern economy relied on massive farms called plantations that grew crops like cotton and tobacco. To make money, these plantations depended entirely on the forced labor of millions of enslaved Black people.
3
Another major cause of the war was an argument over states' rights versus federal power. The Southern states believed that individual states should have the power to make their own laws, especially the right to keep slavery legal. They felt the national (federal) government in Washington, D.C., was getting too strong and interfering with their way of life. The Northern states, however, believed the federal government's laws should be stronger than state laws to keep the country together.
4
The tension reached a breaking point in 1860 with the election of President Abraham Lincoln. Lincoln belonged to the new Republican Party, which wanted to stop slavery from spreading to new western territories. Even though Lincoln did not initially promise to end slavery where it already existed, Southern leaders panicked. They believed Lincoln would eventually destroy their slavery-based economy. In response, eleven Southern states decided to secede, which means to break away or leave the United States. These states formed their own new country called the Confederacy. The Northern states that remained loyal to the U.S. government were called the Union.
5
The Civil War began in 1861 when Confederate soldiers fired on a Union fort. The war was long, bloody, and devastating. At first, President Lincoln's only goal was to defeat the Confederacy and bring the country back together. However, in 1863, Lincoln issued the Emancipation Proclamation. This historic document declared that all enslaved people living in the rebellious Confederate states were officially free. This changed the entire meaning of the war: it was no longer just about saving the Union, but also about destroying the institution of slavery.
6
After four years of brutal fighting, the Union won the war in 1865. The North had more people, more factories to make weapons, and more railroads to move supplies, which gave them a major advantage. As a result of the Union victory, the country was brought back together as one nation. Most importantly, the U.S. government passed the 13th Amendment to the Constitution, which permanently abolished (ended) slavery everywhere in the United States. While the war was over, the difficult period of rebuilding the broken nation, known as Reconstruction, had just begun.
