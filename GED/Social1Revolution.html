Birth of a Nation: The Revolution and the Constitution

Historical Reading Passage
1
In the 1700s, the United States was not an independent country. Instead, it was made up of 13 separate colonies located on the east coast of North America. These colonies were ruled by the British Empire across the ocean in England. For many years, the colonists and the British government had a good relationship. However, this changed when Great Britain fought a costly war and decided to make the American colonists pay for it through new taxes.
2
The colonists were furious, not just because they had to pay money, but because they had no voice in the British government. This led to the famous protest slogan: "No taxation without representation." They believed it was unfair for a king thousands of miles away to demand their money without giving them the right to vote on the laws. This anger and desire for fairness sparked the American Revolution, a war for freedom against British rule.
3
In 1776, a leader named Thomas Jefferson wrote the Declaration of Independence. This historic document officially announced that the 13 colonies were breaking away from Great Britain to form their own country. The Declaration stated that all people are born with basic, natural rights that no government can take away, including the rights to "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."
4
To win their freedom, the colonists had to fight the powerful British army. George Washington, a brilliant and respected leader, took command of the American forces. Despite facing a larger and better-equipped enemy, Washington led the Continental Army to victory. The American Revolution finally ended in 1783, and the United States of America was officially recognized as a free and independent nation.
5
However, winning the war was only the first step. The new country needed a government. At first, they created a system called the Articles of Confederation. Because the Americans were afraid of having another powerful king, they made this first national government extremely weak. It had no president and no power to collect taxes. Very quickly, the founders realized this weak system was failing because the country could not pay its debts or keep order.
6
To fix this problem, leaders from across the country met in Philadelphia in 1787 for the Constitutional Convention. Instead of fixing the old rules, they decided to write a completely new plan for the government: the U.S. Constitution. They wanted a national government that was strong enough to lead the country, but not so strong that it would abuse its power. To achieve this, they divided the government into three separate branches (Executive, Legislative, and Judicial) and created a system of checks and balances. This meant each branch could limit the power of the other two, ensuring no single group had total control.
7
Even with this new balanced system, some people were still worried that the new government might take away their individual freedoms. To calm these fears, the leaders agreed to add the Bill of Rights to the Constitution. The Bill of Rights consists of the first ten amendments (changes) to the Constitution. It guarantees specific personal freedoms, such as the freedom of speech, the freedom of religion, and the right to a fair trial, which Americans still cherish today.
